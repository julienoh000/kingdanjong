Have you heard of The King's Warden?

Released in 2026, it became one of the most talked-about Korean historical films in years — a quiet, devastating story about an ordinary man who chose loyalty over survival. Set in 15th-century Joseon, it follows a local magistrate who secretly buries a deposed king with his own hands, at the risk of everything he has.

But here's what makes it extraordinary: it actually happened. Behind the film lies a real king, a real act of defiance, and a story that the Joseon court tried to erase from history for decades. Scroll down — we'll tell you the truth behind the screen.

1441–1457

King Danjong

Danjong was the sixth king of the Joseon Dynasty and is remembered as the most sorrowful figure in Korean history. Crowned at the young age of 12, his reign was cut short by a bloody coup led by his own uncle, King Sejo. Deposed and exiled to a remote village in Yeongwol, Danjong’s life was a series of lonely struggles. His story is not just about a fallen king, but a testament to unwavering loyalty—represented by those who risked everything to protect him, even in death.

1452 · Enthronement

A Child King Takes the Throne

Following the sudden death of his father, King Munjong, 12-year-old Danjong became the sixth King of Joseon. Too young to rule alone, he was placed under the protection of loyal ministers, but his throne was already surrounded by the shadows of ambitious relatives.

1453 · Coup of Gyeyu

The Bloody Coup

Danjong’s uncle, Grand Prince Suyang, launched a violent coup to seize power. He eliminated the King’s most trusted guardians, including General Kim Jong-seo, claiming they were "cleansing" the court. After this event, Danjong became a king in name only, losing all political power to his uncle.

1455 · Forced Abdication

Stepping Down in Shadow

Under immense pressure and threats to his life, Danjong was forced to give up his crown to his uncle, who then became King Sejo. Danjong was demoted to "Sang-wang" (Retired King) and moved to a smaller palace, living a life of isolation and constant surveillance.
